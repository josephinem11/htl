What College Students Should be Eating for Increased Happiness

The concept of a 'happiness diet' focused on regular, nutritious meals and snacks presents a promising strategy for enhancing the quality of life among college students and the broader population.

In recent years, the link between happiness, health, and diet has captivated the interest of researchers across the globe. A cross-sectional web-based study conducted at Qazvin University of Medical Sciences delves into this connection, exploring how dietary habits such as breakfast consumption and intake of fruits and vegetables correlate with levels of happiness among students.

The Study at a Glance:

  • Participants: The study engaged 541 students (75.4% female, 24.5% male) from Qazvin University of Medical Sciences, with an age range of 18–46 years old. The response rate was 49.82%, with participation being voluntary and anonymous.
  • Measuring Happiness: Happiness levels were gauged using the Oxford Happiness Questionnaire (OHQ), which consists of 29 statements rated on a scale from ‘strongly disagree’ to ‘strongly agree’. The reliability and validity of the OHQ have been previously confirmed among university students in Iran.
  • Dietary Habits and Physical Activity: Students reported their dietary habits, including breakfast, fruit, and vegetable consumption, through a detailed web-based questionnaire. Physical activity was assessed using the Rapid Assessment of Physical Activity (RAPA), a self-administered questionnaire evaluating a range of activity levels.

Key Findings:

  • Breakfast is Crucial: The study underscored the importance of breakfast consumption, associating it with higher happiness scores. Students who regularly ate breakfast (7 days a week) reported the highest levels of happiness, while those who skipped breakfast had the lowest.
  • Fruits and Vegetables Matter: A significant relationship was found between the daily intake of fruits and vegetables and happiness. Students consuming 4 servings or more daily of each food group were the happiest.
  • Meal Frequency Counts: Happiness was also linked to the number of meals eaten daily. Students having 3 meals and 1-2 snacks per day reported the highest happiness levels, whereas those with fewer meals and snacks had lower happiness scores.

Implications and Insights:

This study not only confirms the significant role of nutrition in physical and mental health but also highlights the potential of dietary habits in enhancing psychological well-being. The findings suggest that regular consumption of nutritious breakfast and higher intake of fruits and vegetables are associated with improved happiness among students. Moreover, the frequency of meals and snacks throughout the day plays a critical role in sustaining mood.

While previous research has largely focused on the relationship between negative emotions and food intake, this study sheds light on the positive impact of diet on mood. Biochemical studies have indicated that certain micronutrients found in fruits and vegetables may contribute to a sense of well-being and happiness by improving cognitive function and mood.

A Call for Nutrition Education:

Given the link between diet quality and psychological well-being, there is a pressing need for nutrition education strategies, especially among young and adolescent populations. By promoting healthier dietary choices, such programs could potentially enhance both mental and physical health, leading to a happier and healthier society.

Concluding Thoughts:

The study’s findings emphasize the bidirectional relationship between happiness and dietary habits. While positive emotions can foster healthier eating behaviors, nutritious food choices can also promote positive feelings and overall happiness. As we continue to unravel the complex interactions between diet and mood, the prospect of a 'happiness diet' focused on breakfast, fruits, and vegetables offers a promising avenue for boosting psychological well-being alongside physical health.

Citation:

Lesani, A., Mohammadpoorasl, A., Javadi, M. et al. (2016). Eating breakfast, fruit and vegetable intake and their relation with happiness in college students.  Eat Weight Disord  21, 645–651. https://doi.org/10.1007/s40519-016-0261-0
